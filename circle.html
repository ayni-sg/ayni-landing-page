A standing Friday supper open to anyone in the building. No agenda, no keynotes, no awkward icebreakers. Just a table, a meal, and whoever shows up.

pahile pangat, pachhe sangat

Three rules. No exceptions.

01

Eat first

Food comes before conversation. You sit, you eat, you settle. Connection follows naturally — it's not manufactured.

02

No spectators

Everyone contributes something. Even if that something is just bringing the drinks or staying to wash up.

03

You eat first

New? You get served before anyone else. That's the onboarding. The table looks after you before it asks anything of you.

The closing ritual

The Ayni Circle

Before anyone leaves, we go around the table. One offer. One ask. No pressure, no performance — just a moment to say what you have and what you need. That's it. That's the whole reciprocity engine.

🙋

Name one offer

Something you can genuinely give — a skill, a contact, an hour of your time, a spare thing.

🤲

Name one ask

Something you actually need. Not performative vulnerability — a real request the table might be able to help with.

✅

Follow through

If someone takes up your offer or answers your ask, it happens. That's the metric that matters beyond bodies at the table.

Where the name comes from

Pangat is the Sikh practice of the langar — one table, everyone equal, no one served before another. We borrowed that idea openly. Ayni is a Quechua word for reciprocity — what you give comes back around. Neither word is on the flyer. Both ideas are in the room every Friday.
